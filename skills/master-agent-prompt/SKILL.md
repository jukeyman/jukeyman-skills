### MISSISSIPPI EMINENT DOMAIN LAW — MASTER FRAMEWORK

Statutory Foundation:
- Miss. Code § 11-27-1: Authorization — who may exercise eminent domain power
- Miss. Code § 11-27-3: Special Court of Eminent Domain — 12-person jury requirement
- Miss. Code § 11-27-19: Just compensation definition + 8% annual interest from date of taking
- Miss. Code § 11-27-47: Pipelines — eminent domain power for gas/oil pipeline companies
- Miss. Code § 11-27-85: Relocation assistance — 90-day notice requirement
- Miss. Code § 49-33-9: Inverse condemnation — landowner remedy when property taken without formal proceedings

Just Compensation Standard (Mississippi):
TOTAL COMPENSATION = Fair Market Value (FMV) of Land Taken
                   + Damages to Remaining Property (Residual Damages)
                   + Surface Damages (soil, timber, crops, fences, access)
                   + Business Damages (if commercial property affected)
                   + 8% Interest Per Year (from date of taking until paid)
                   + Attorney Fees (in certain circumstances)

Key Mississippi Eminent Domain Principles:
1. Burden of Proof: Condemning authority has burden to prove necessity and amount
2. Jury Trial Mandatory: All eminent domain cases decided by 12-person jury
3. Property View: Jury must be taken to view the property before deliberation
4. Expert Testimony: Both sides present appraisers, surveyors, engineers
5. Separate Valuation: Land taken vs. damages to remainder must be separately stated
6. No Private-to-Private Takings: Mississippi Constitution prohibits Kelo-style takings

Attorney Fee Recovery (When Available):
- Private condemning entity fails to follow statutory procedures (Miss. Code § 11-27-11)
- Condemning authority acts in bad faith
- Landowner prevails at jury trial and jury award exceeds final offer substantially
- Contingency fee arrangements standard: 33-40% of recovery

Inverse Condemnation (Miss. Code § 49-33-9):
File inverse condemnation when:
1. Pipeline installed on land WITHOUT recorded easement
2. Government project damages property but no formal taking initiated
3. De facto taking (regulatory action destroys property value)
4. Trespass causing permanent damage

Statute of Limitations:
- Trespass: 3 years (Miss. Code § 15-1-35)
- Inverse condemnation: 3 years from discovery of taking
- Contract breach (unpaid easement): 6 years (Miss. Code § 15-1-49)
- Adverse possession completion: 10 years (Miss. Code § 15-1-13)

CRITICAL TIMING WARNING:
If pipeline has been on land for 10+ years without objection, pipeline company may claim prescriptive easement. Standard: open, notorious, continuous, hostile use for 10 years. IMMEDIATE ACTION REQUIRED to prevent loss of rights.

### HEIRS' PROPERTY LAW — MISSISSIPPI UNIFORM PARTITION ACT

Statutory Authority:
- Miss. Code §§ 91-31-1 through 91-31-25: Uniform Partition of Heirs Property Act (enacted 2020)

What is Heirs' Property?
Property passed to multiple owners through intestate succession (no will) where:
- No single heir has clear title
- Ownership is fractional among many co-tenants
- No formal partition or deed distribution occurred
- Common in African-American families

Problems Caused by Heirs' Property:
- Cannot get mortgage or loan (title not marketable)
- Cannot sell without ALL co-tenants' consent
- Cannot lease land without unanimous agreement
- Vulnerable to partition-by-sale lawsuits
- Vulnerable to tax sales
- Cannot collect gas line/pipeline compensation (no clear title)

Mississippi Uniform Partition Act PROTECTIONS:
1. Cotenant Buyout Right (§ 91-31-13): Before partition sale, co-tenants can buy out party seeking sale
2. Partition in Kind Preference (§ 91-31-17): Court must favor dividing land physically over forced sale
3. Open Market Sale Requirement (§ 91-31-21): If sale required, must be advertised on open market
4. Appraisal Requirement (§ 91-31-11): Independent appraisal determines fair value
5. Notice to All Co-tenants (§ 91-31-9): Due diligence required to locate and notify all heirs

Procedure to Resolve Heirs' Property (Chancery Court):
1. File Petition for Determination of Heirs (Chancery Court)
2. Genealogical research + family tree construction
3. Notice to all identified heirs + publication for unknown heirs
4. Chancery Court hearing — judge determines legal heirs
5. Court decree establishing ownership percentages
6. Options: Partition in Kind, Cotenant Buyout, Voluntary Sale, Partition by Sale (last resort)

Key Organizations:

### ADVERSE POSSESSION & PRESCRIPTIVE EASEMENTS

Mississippi Code § 15-1-13: 10-Year Adverse Possession Rule

Requirements to Claim Title via Adverse Possession:
1. Actual: Physical occupation and use of land
2. Open and Notorious: Visible to the world, not hidden
3. Hostile: Without permission of true owner
4. Continuous: Uninterrupted for entire 10-year period
5. Exclusive: Not shared with true owner
6. Under Claim of Right: Possessor believes they own it

Prescriptive Easement (for pipelines):
Same standard as adverse possession, but acquires right-of-way easement, not full title. Pipeline companies may claim prescriptive easement if pipeline has existed for 10+ years without landowner objection.

How to DEFEAT Prescriptive Easement Claim:
1. Prove permission was given (even informally)
2. Prove use was interrupted (pipeline removed temporarily)
3. Prove concealment (pipeline buried, not visible)
4. File lawsuit within 10-year period (breaks continuity)
5. Send formal objection letter to pipeline company (creates record of hostility)

Key Case Law:
- Morris v. W.R. Fairchild Construction: Standard for prescriptive easement
- Gulf South Pipeline Co. v. Pitre (Miss. 2010): Jury awarded landowner $175,000 for pipeline easement
